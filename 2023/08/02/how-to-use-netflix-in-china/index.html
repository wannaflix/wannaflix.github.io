Bypassing Internet Censorship in Mainland China: A Comprehensive Guide

When you’re in mainland China, or planning a trip there, one thing that you’re sure to consider in advance - perhaps the biggest challenge you’ll face - is internet censorship. The Chinese government has implemented stringent internet restrictions. Most of the mainstream social networks you use daily, like Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and WhatsApp, along with streaming platforms like Netflix, Hulu, HBO, and YouTube, and even the search engine Google, are all inaccessible in mainland China. You might not be too worried about this. Maybe you’ve already planned ahead and purchased some VPN services like ExpressVPN, NordVPN, etc. However, once you actually arrive in mainland China, you’ll find that these pricey, popular services are surprisingly ineffective. In some places they work intermittently, while in others they drop out every few minutes, and the speed leaves much to be desired. It’s a pretty dismal situation.

You might be wondering why these big-name services don’t work as expected. This is because VPNs weren’t originally designed to bypass China’s internet censorship. Their primary purpose is to ensure user privacy by creating a secure ‘tunnel’, as the name ‘Virtual Private Network’ suggests. Because of this characteristic, VPNs are easily detected by China’s Great Firewall, which will immediately cut your connection if it detects VPN usage. Method 1: Non-Mainland China SIM Card

There’s a very simple solution: the mobile SIM card you bring with you to China. If you use it to access the internet in mainland China, there are no restrictions. You can directly visit the websites you like. But this doesn’t apply to SIM cards obtained in China. The major drawback of this method is its cost. It’s very expensive to roam internationally. Unless you use a service like Google Fi, which is somewhat cheaper, this method is still costly and usually comes with significant data limitations.

Using Netflix, HBO, and Other Streaming Services in Mainland China is More Challenging than Using Google

Why is there a difference, even though both involve bypassing internet censorship?

Take Netflix as an example. Most of its content is subject to copyright restrictions, which are related to your location when watching. Unlike Apple’s Apple ID, Netflix does not recommend content based on the account’s location, but rather on the login location, which is determined by the network environment.

If you log in from the U.S., you’ll be recommended content available in the U.S. If you log in from Japan, you’ll see content from Japan. Because of this, Netflix has implemented measures to prevent cross-regional usage via VPNs or other proxy methods. Once it detects that you’re currently using a VPN or proxy, it will only recommend content that has global copyrights, or even directly block your account.

What’s even more disappointing is that Netflix doesn’t provide services in mainland China, and mainland China blocks access to Netflix. This means that in mainland China, you need to break through a double blockade to watch content normally. This imposes stricter requirements on service providers. Most proxy service providers do not support region-specific content viewing on Netflix.
